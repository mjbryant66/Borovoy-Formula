# On the Comparative Reliability of LLM-Assisted Scholarship

Error is pervasive in every epistemic enterprise. Biomedical literature audits document citation errors in roughly 24% of references and misquotation of the cited authority in 15%;[^1] psychology replication projects recover 36% of canonical studies, experimental economics 61%, social-science experiments in *Nature* and *Science* 62%, and preclinical cancer biology approximately 40% of positive findings — the last number collected, critically, from only 50 of 193 planned experiments because the original methods sections were too incomplete to replicate;[^2] Simkin and Roychowdhury estimate from citation-error propagation patterns that roughly 78% of citers never read the source they cite;[^3] Fanelli's meta-analysis records 1.97% of scientists admitting data fabrication or falsification and 33.7% admitting other questionable research practices;[^4] and Retraction Watch documented more than 10,000 formal retractions in 2023 alone, in a database exceeding 63,000 entries.[^5] Traditional journalism fares similarly: Maier's cross-market audit of fourteen U.S. newspapers found objective factual errors in 48% of stories.[^6] Large language models used without retrieval hallucinate on 58% (GPT-4) to 88% (Llama 2) of specific-case legal queries, and retrieval-augmented commercial legal tools reduce but do not eliminate the problem — Lexis+ AI and Ask Practical Law AI approximately 17%, Westlaw AI-Assisted Research approximately 34%;[^7] the October 2025 EBU–BBC cross-market audit of AI news assistants across twenty-two public-service broadcasters in eighteen countries found 45% of answers had at least one significant issue, 31% had serious sourcing problems, and 20% had major accuracy problems.[^8] The meaningful observation is not that LLMs err — both LLMs and humans do, at rates of the same order of magnitude — but that LLM errors are reproducible, auditable, and inter-model-verifiable by construction, while peer review catches only about one-third of deliberately inserted major methodological errors even when reviewers are specifically trained for the task.[^9] It is therefore at least arguable that a well-instrumented LLM pipeline — with published prompt, model version, sampling parameters, and cross-model verification — has a clearer path to catching its own errors than the human scholarly ecosystem has historically achieved at catching its own.

[^2]: Open Science Collaboration, "Estimating the Reproducibility of Psychological Science" (2015) 349 *Science* aac4716, https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aac4716 (36% replication across 100 studies); Colin F. Errington et al., "Investigating the Replicability of Preclinical Cancer Biology" (2021) 10 *eLife* e71601, https://doi.org/10.7554/eLife.71601 (~40% of positive-result experiments from 50 of 193 planned replications completed).

[^3]: M.V. Simkin & V.P. Roychowdhury, "Read Before You Cite!" (2003) 14 *Complex Systems* 269, https://www.complex-systems.com/abstracts/v14_i03_a05/; preprint at https://arxiv.org/abs/cond-mat/0212043.

[^5]: Richard Van Noorden, "More Than 10,000 Research Papers Were Retracted in 2023 — A New Record" *Nature* (12 December 2023), https://doi.org/10.1038/d41586-023-03974-8; Retraction Watch Database, http://retractiondatabase.org.

[^7]: Matthew Dahl, Varun Magesh, Mirac Suzgun & Daniel E. Ho, "Large Legal Fictions: Profiling Legal Hallucinations in Large Language Models" (2024) 16 *Journal of Legal Analysis* 64, https://doi.org/10.1093/jla/laae003, preprint at https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.01301 (bare-model range 58–88%); Varun Magesh, Faiz Surani, Matthew Dahl, Mirac Suzgun, Christopher D. Manning & Daniel E. Ho, "Hallucination-Free? [^8]: European Broadcasting Union & BBC, *News Integrity in AI Assistants* (October 2025), https://www.ebu.ch/Report/MIS-BBC/NI_AI_2025.pdf (22 public-service broadcasters, 18 countries, 14 languages, >3,000 responses tested).

[^9]: Sara Schroter, Nick Black, Stephen Evans, Fiona Godlee, Lyda Osorio & Richard Smith, "What Errors Do Peer Reviewers Detect, and Does Training Improve Their Ability to Detect Them?"
